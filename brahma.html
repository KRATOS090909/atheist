The Lie of the Infinite Pillar

(The Jyotirlinga Incident)

This is the most direct "wrongdoing" involving a deliberate lie to claim superiority among the Gods.

The Incident

Brahma and Vishnu fought over who was supreme. Shiva appeared as an endless pillar of fire (Jyotirlinga) and challenged them to find its ends.

The Wrongdoing

Unable to find the top, Brahma deliberately lied, claiming he had reached the summit, and produced a false witness to corroborate his deceit.

Exact Proof

Shiva Purana, Vidyesvara Samhita, Chapters 6–8.

Chapter 7, Verse 28–30:

Brahma finds the Ketaki flower falling from the top and convinces it to lie for him.

Chapter 8, Verse 1–5:

Brahma tells Vishnu he reached the top. Shiva then manifests from the pillar, exposing Brahma's deceit.

Consequence

Shiva cursed Brahma, declaring that he would never have a place in temples or be worshipped by the public again.

Infatuation with His Own Creation

(Shatarupa/Saraswati)

This incident highlights Brahma's inability to control his senses toward his "daughter" (creation).

The Incident

Brahma created a beautiful woman named Shatarupa (also called Savitri or Saraswati) to assist in creation.

The Wrongdoing

He became so infatuated that he sprouted four heads to follow her movements and a fifth head when she tried to fly above him to escape his gaze.

Exact Proof

Matsya Purana, Chapter 3, Verses 32–43.

Verse 33–34:

Brahma's infatuation is described as he keeps staring at her beauty.

Verse 40–42:

Mentions the sprouting of the fifth head as she tried to ascend to heaven.

Consequence

For this "licentious" and "unholy" behavior, Shiva's form Bhairava cut off the fifth head.

The Replacement of Saraswati

(The Gayatri Incident)

Brahma's impatience during a sacred ritual led to a curse from his first wife.

The Incident

Brahma was performing a grand Yajna (sacrifice) at Pushkar. The ritual required his wife, Saraswati, to be present.

The Wrongdoing

When Saraswati was delayed, Brahma grew impatient and married a milkmaid named Gayatri on the spot to continue the ritual without her.

Exact Proof

Padma Purana, Shrishti Khanda, Chapter 17.

Verse 155–160:

Describes Brahma's anger at the delay and his hasty marriage to Gayatri.

Verse 180–185:

Saraswati arrives, sees another woman in her seat, and curses Brahma.

Consequence

Saraswati cursed him, stating he would be worshipped only in Pushkar and nowhere else on Earth.

The Disrespect of Sage Bhrigu

(Bhrigu's Test of the Trimurti)

This incident demonstrates Brahma's ego (Rajoguna) getting the better of him.

The Incident

Sage Bhrigu was sent to test which of the Trinity (Brahma, Vishnu, Shiva) was most worthy of being the supreme deity for a sacrifice.

The Wrongdoing

When Bhrigu arrived at Brahma-loka, Brahma was so absorbed in his own importance (or music) that he did not stand up or offer the customary welcome to the sage.

Exact Proof

Padma Purana, Uttara Khanda, Chapter 255 (also mentioned in Bhagavata Purana, Canto 10, Chapter 89).

Verse 12–15:

Bhrigu notes Brahma's arrogance and total lack of hospitality.

Consequence

Bhrigu cursed Brahma, declaring that because he was filled with "foulness" (ego), no Brahmin would ever offer him worship.

The Theft of Cowherd Boys

(Brahma-vimohana-lila)

In this incident, Brahma attempted to test the divinity of Lord Krishna by kidnapping his friends and cattle.

The Incident

After Krishna killed the demon Aghasura, Brahma was curious to see if this child was truly the Supreme Lord.

The Wrongdoing

He used his mystical powers to steal and hide all of Krishna's cowherd friends and their calves in a cave.

Exact Proof

Srimad Bhagavata Purana, Canto 10, Chapter 13.

Verse 15:

Describes how Brahma led the boys and calves away to a secret place while Krishna was looking for them.

Consequence

Krishna simply expanded himself into identical forms of all the boys and calves for a full year until Brahma realized his mistake and surrendered.

Lustful Pursuit of Sandhya

(The Daughter of Mind)

In some Kalpas (aeons), the story of Brahma's infatuation involves a deity named Sandhya (the Twilight).

The Incident

Brahma created Sandhya and immediately became overwhelmed by desire for her.

The Wrongdoing

He pursued her relentlessly, ignoring the moral boundaries between a creator and his creation.

Exact Proof

Siva Purana, Rudra-Samhita, Sati-Khanda, Chapter 2–5 (also referenced in Bhagavata Purana 3.31.36).

SB 3.31.36:

States that Brahma was bewildered by the charms of his own daughter and shamelessly pursued her.

Consequence

This behavior led the other gods and sages to condemn him, and in many versions, Shiva intervened to punish this "licentious" behavior.

Insulting Lord Shiva's "Beggar" Form

(Insulting Lord Shiva)

There are accounts where Brahma's arrogance led him to insult Shiva's appearance, which resulted in immediate punishment.

The Incident

During a meeting of the gods, Shiva appeared in a fierce or "unrefined" form.

The Wrongdoing

Brahma, filled with pride, mocked Shiva and claimed he was the only true supreme father, treating Shiva as a lowly subordinate.

Exact Proof

Shiva Purana, Satarudra-samhita, Chapter 8.

Verse Proof:

Brahma's fifth head is described as "insolent" and constantly speaking ill of the divine.

Consequence

Shiva manifested as Kaal Bhairav and used his thumbnail to slice off the offending fifth head as a lesson in humility.

Creation of Hunger and Fear

(Creation of Hunger and Fear)

In a more philosophical critique found in certain Puranic commentaries, Brahma is viewed as the source of "biological" problems.

The Incident

Brahma created the material world and the physical bodies of living beings.

The "Wrongdoing"

Scholars like Devdutt Pattanaik note that by creating organic life, Brahma also created hunger, desire, and fear of death.

Theological Argument

Unlike Vishnu (who preserves) and Shiva (who liberates), Brahma is the one who "traps" souls in the cycle of physical suffering and biological need.

Consequence

Because he is the "Creator of Bondage," many schools of thought suggest he is not worshipped as a savior, but rather seen as the one who initiated the struggle for survival.

The Creation of the Asuras (Demons)

(Creation of Asuras from Thighs)

Brahma is often critiqued for creating the "darker" aspects of existence, sometimes by mistake or through a lack of self-control.

The Incident

During the process of creation, Brahma became frustrated and filled with darkness.

The Action

He created the Asuras (demons) from his thighs. Immediately after, he became so lustful for a female form that he had to abandon his own body.

Exact Proof

Srimad Bhagavata Purana, Canto 3, Chapter 20, Verses 21-23.

Verse 22:

Describes Brahma giving up the body that was "overtaken by hunger and anger" and "darkened by passion."

Consequence

This act populated the universe with chaotic forces that constantly war with the gods.

Cursing his Son, Narada

(Life of Lust Curse)

Brahma's anger often flared when his children chose spiritual paths over the physical work of creation.

The Incident

Brahma ordered his son, Sage Narada, to marry and help populate the world.

The Wrongdoing

When Narada refused, wanting to stay a celibate devotee, Brahma lost his temper and used his power to punish his own offspring for seeking enlightenment.

Exact Proof

Narada Purana, Part 1, Chapter 3.

Verses 50-55:

Brahma curses Narada to become a "Gandharva" (a celestial singer) known for his lustful nature, stripping him of his wisdom for a time.

Consequence

Narada cursed Brahma back, stating that Brahma would never be worshipped and his wisdom would always be clouded by desire.

The Arrogance of "The First Born"

(The Ego of Creation)

Brahma frequently claimed he was the only creator, ignoring that he himself originated from the navel of Vishnu.

The Incident

In various meetings of the gods, Brahma spoke with extreme ego, claiming to be the supreme master of all.

The Wrongdoing

He claimed that even Shiva and Vishnu were his subordinates, which disturbed the balance of the universe and showed a complete lack of humility.

Exact Proof

Kurma Purana, Purva-bhaga, Chapter 9.

Verses 6-10:

Brahma argues with Vishnu, claiming, "I am the self-born, the master of the universe; you are born from me."

Consequence

Shiva appeared and had to humble Brahma by showing him his true place in the cosmic hierarchy.

Giving the "Atma-Linga" to Ravana

(Granting Boons to Evil)

Brahma's most frequent "wrong" is his lack of foresight when granting boons to evil beings.

The Incident

The demon king Ravana performed penance to Brahma to gain invincibility.

The Action

Despite knowing Ravana's evil intent, Brahma gave him incredible power and directed him toward Shiva to obtain the Atma-Linga (the soul of Shiva), knowing full well the havoc Ravana would cause.

Exact Proof

Shiva Purana, Kotirudra-samhita, Chapter 28.

Chapter Summary:

This chapter describes Brahma's role in empowering Ravana, which led to the near-destruction of the world until Vishnu intervened as Rama.

Consequence

Ravana used this power to terrorize the three worlds, proving that Brahma's boons often prioritized ritualistic rules over the greater moral good.

Cursing the Sages for Questioning Him

(Sensitivity to Criticism)

Brahma was known to be sensitive to criticism from his own created sages (Rishis).

The Incident

When the sages questioned Brahma's infatuation with the woman Shatarupa, they called his behavior "un-Vedic" and immoral.

The Action

Instead of repenting, Brahma became furious and cursed his own sons (the Manasaputras) for their "insolence" in pointing out his flaws.

Exact Proof

Skanda Purana, Mahesvara Khanda, Chapter 35.

Chapter Summary:

This highlights Brahma's inability to take moral advice, leading to friction within the divine family and further questioning of his supreme status.

Consequence

This event showed that Brahma often valued his own ego and desire over the righteous guidance of the sages, further alienating him from divine worship.
